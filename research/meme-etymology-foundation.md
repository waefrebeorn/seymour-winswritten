## I. Etymology of "Meme"

**Origin:** Coined by Richard Dawkins, *The Selfish Gene* (1976), Chapter 11: "Memes: The New Replicators"

**Greek root:** μίμημα (*mīmēma*) — "imitated thing"
- From μιμεῖσθαι (*mimeisthai*) — "to imitate"
- From μῖμος (*mimos*) — "mime, imitator"

**Dawkins' construction:** Deliberately modeled on "gene" — a replicator of cultural information analogous to the gene as a replicator of biological information.

**Key distinction:** Dawkins intended "meme" as a *theoretical* unit of cultural transmission, not specifically internet content. The internet sense is a later narrowing/popularization.

## II. Dawkins' Original Examples of Memes (1976)

- Melodies and musical phrases
- Catchphrases and slogans
- Fashion (clothing styles, hairstyles)
- Technology (the technique of building archways)
- Religious ideas and rituals
- Proverbs and folk wisdom

## IV. The Two Memetics

### A. Classical Memetics (1976–2000)
- Memes as *cultural genes*
- Focus on replication fidelity, fecundity, longevity
- Controversial: criticized as pseudoscience, unfalsifiable
- Key claim: memes drive cultural evolution the same way genes drive biological evolution

### B. Internet Meme Studies (2000–present)
- Memes as *participatory cultural units*
- Focus on remix, virality, platform dynamics
- Empirical: can be studied via data analysis
- Key claim: internet memes are a new form of public discourse

**Seymour's position:** We treat both as valid. The Dawkins framework provides the *theoretical* foundation; internet meme studies provide the *empirical* material. The Ponderables live in the overlap.

## V. Key Concepts for the Ponderables

### Memeplex (Dawkins)
A group of memes that replicate together — e.g., a religion, a political ideology, a scientific paradigm. In Seymour's world: each Ponderable is a designed memeplex.

### Meme Mutation
Memes change as they spread. Internet memes mutate faster than any previous cultural form because digital copying is trivial. Each mutation is a choice — what to keep, what to change.

### Attention as Selection Pressure
In the digital age, attention replaces survival as the primary selection pressure for memes. Memes that capture attention replicate; those that don't, die.

### Context Collapse (danah boyd)
When content moves between social contexts, its meaning changes. A meme in one community means something different in another. This is the Colonel's "create context, not control content" made real.

### Platform Determinism
The medium shapes the meme. Twitter memes are different from TikTok memes not because of content but because of platform architecture. The S3 Plan is the platform.

## VI. Seymour's Meme Taxonomy (Working Draft)

1. **Foundational Memes** — Ideas so embedded they're invisible (language, money, time)
2. **Viral Memes** — Fast-spreading, short-lived cultural units (challenges, formats, jokes)
3. **Institutional Memes** — Memes embedded in organizations and systems (brands, bureaucracies, traditions)
4. **Meta-Memes** — Memes about memes (meme culture itself, "meme awareness")
5. **Dormant Memes** — Ideas that failed to spread but persist in archives (forgotten theories, dead languages)
6. **Engineered Memes** — Deliberately designed for spread (propaganda, advertising, the S3 Plan)

## VII. The Colonel's Thesis (as Memetic Theory)

The Colonel's argument in MGS2 is essentially a memetic theory of governance:

1. Information accumulates without selection
2. Unfiltered information slows cultural evolution
3. Therefore, filtering is necessary for progress
4. The Patriots (AIs) are the optimal filters
5. Human compliance with filtered information is the measure of success

**Seymour's counter-thesis:** The filter IS the meme. The Patriots don't filter memes — they *become* the dominant memeplex. Curation is colonization.
